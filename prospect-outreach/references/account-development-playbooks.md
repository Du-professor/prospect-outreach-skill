# Account Development Playbooks

Load this reference for Deep mode, contact-channel recommendations, reply handling, or meeting preparation.

## Opportunity Paths

Select one primary path and no more than two credible secondary paths.

| Path | Positive evidence | Common disqualifier |
|---|---|---|
| End customer | Prospect operates a workflow where the user's demonstrated offering could be used directly. | Prospect only advises others and does not buy or specify the category. |
| OEM supply | Prospect builds equipment or products requiring the relevant component or platform. | No visible design ownership or technical integration need. |
| Systems-integrator partnership | Prospect delivers automation or engineering projects and may specify platforms for customers. | It is tied exclusively to another platform or has no relevant project scope. |
| Channel or distributor | Prospect has regional sales, service, inventory, or partner capability for complementary products. | No channel operation, technical support capacity, or market overlap. |
| Technical partnership | Both parties have complementary engineering capabilities but a buying path is not yet clear. | Capabilities duplicate each other without a joint value proposition. |
| Long-term nurture | Strategic relevance exists but evidence, timing, contactability, or consent is insufficient. | A clear mismatch or do-not-contact status makes monitoring unnecessary. |

For each path state the value exchange, buyer or partner role, evidence, commercial unknowns, and first validation question.

## Product Mapping

Map each proposed fit through this chain:

`Prospect workflow -> visible requirement or hypothesis -> demonstrated user-company capability -> proof -> gap/risk -> verification question`

Do not map a category name directly to another category name. High-confidence mappings require both a specific prospect workflow and user-company proof.

## Buying-Committee Map

Use role families rather than guessed names.

| Role family | Likely interest | Influence | Likely objection | Routing or discovery question |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Engineering / R&D | Technical fit, integration, performance, lifecycle | Evaluator or owner | Compatibility and engineering effort | Who owns platform or component evaluation for this workflow? |
| Automation / Controls / IT-OT | Architecture, protocols, manageability, cybersecurity | Technical influencer or owner | Integration and support risk | Is this evaluated by controls, OT, or equipment engineering? |
| Operations / Maintenance | Reliability, serviceability, downtime, spares | User and influencer | Change risk and maintainability | Which operating constraint matters most in current projects? |
| Procurement / Supply Chain | Supplier qualification, price, lead time, continuity | Commercial gatekeeper | Qualification and supply risk | At what stage does procurement join a technical evaluation? |
| Product / Business Unit | Customer value, roadmap, differentiation | Sponsor or owner | Market relevance and roadmap fit | Is this part of an active product or partner roadmap? |
| Channel / Partnerships | Portfolio fit, territory, support, margin | Partnership owner | Conflict and enablement burden | Who reviews new technology or supplier partnerships? |
| Executive | Strategic fit, risk, growth | Sponsor | Priority and resource commitment | Which team should validate the opportunity before executive review? |

## Contact-Channel Recommendation

Rank only channels that are publicly available and business-relevant:

1. Named business contact whose public role directly owns the topic.
2. Public role or department inbox for the relevant function.
3. Public regional office or business-unit inbox.
4. Public general contact used for routing.

For each available option state role fit, source, advantage, limitation, and recommended message posture. Never infer an unpublished individual email pattern. When only a general inbox exists, use a routing question and do not address a guessed person.

## Reply Playbooks

When the user provides a prospect reply, stop the planned sequence and draft one context-aware response with one CTA.

- **Interested:** confirm the specific topic, suggest a small next step, and avoid expanding into the full catalog.
- **Requests information:** ask which application or criteria matter, then offer only the relevant material.
- **Wrong person:** thank them and ask once for the correct role or department.
- **Not now:** acknowledge timing, ask permission for a specific future month or trigger, and record a pause.
- **Price question:** explain that scope affects pricing and ask for the minimum technical or quantity context needed; do not evade a price the user has authorized.
- **Explicit refusal or opt-out:** acknowledge briefly, stop, and mark `do not contact`.
- **Out of office:** record the return date and do not send another message before it.

Do not invent attachments, prices, availability, meeting commitments, or technical answers not supplied by the user.

## Meeting Preparation

When a prospect asks to meet or visit, first clarify:

- Online or on-site
- Proposed date, time, duration, and time zone
- Online platform or physical address
- Attendees and roles
- Main topics and desired outcome
- Language and interpreter needs
- Site-access, safety, confidentiality, or visitor requirements when relevant

If the platform is unspecified, ask which platform they prefer and optionally offer only platforms the user has confirmed are available.

Use this default 30-minute agenda:

| Time | Topic |
|---:|---|
| 0-5 min | Introductions, company context, and meeting objective |
| 5-15 min | Prospect priorities, workflow, constraints, and current approach |
| 15-23 min | Discuss only the most relevant capability or cooperation hypothesis |
| 23-28 min | Qualification gaps, responsibilities, and possible next step |
| 28-30 min | Confirm owners, materials, timing, and follow-up |

Prepare 5-8 discovery questions covering application, current architecture, evaluation criteria, integration constraints, decision roles, timing, supplier requirements, and next step. After the meeting, draft a factual recap with decisions, open questions, owners, dates, and promised materials.

## Reactivation Conditions

For P3, P4, paused, or nurture accounts, state observable reasons to revisit, such as a relevant product launch, facility expansion, hiring signal, tender, partner announcement, leadership request, new public contact role, or user-supplied project context. Assign a next-review date instead of forcing an email.
